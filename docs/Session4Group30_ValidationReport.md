Prior to our usability testing in Weeks 5–8, we completed Stage 1 (Problem Validation) during Week 4. We conducted deep interviews with 5 students to confirm that the "information vacuum" and commercial bias on generic platforms are genuine pain points in the Taicang campus area . This current report focuses on Stage 3 (Usability Testing) to validate our solution's logic and interface.

# What you asked them to do

We prepared three specific, immersive tasks rather than general questions: 

1. Identity Access: "You heard this platform is exclusively for XJTLU students. Try to create a new account and log in using your university credentials." 

2. Intelligent Discovery: "Imagine you need a quiet spot for coding with spicy food. Use the AI assistant to find the top 3 recommendations." 

3. Community Contribution: "You just had an amazing meal at 'Taicang Bistro.' Post a recommendation with a photo, ensuring others know it is 'Good for Group Study'." 

# How you captured what happened

To ensure high-quality data collection, we used a multi-modal recording approach for each 20-minute session: 

Audio & Screen Recording: With explicit verbal consent, we recorded the audio and the participant’s screen interactions to capture "Think Aloud" moments and navigation paths. 

• Structured Observation Sheets: A dedicated team member acted as an observer, noting down "First Click" behavior, points of hesitation, and any features the user skipped. 

Post-Session Interview: A 3-minute debrief followed each task to clarify why certain errors occurred (e.g., "What did you expect to happen when you clicked this?"). * Storage: All raw recordings and handwritten notes have been digitized and stored in our team's shared SharePoint folder, referenced as Appendix A1–A6 below . 

# Section 2 — What You Found

Finding 1: Erosion of Trust via External Entry Points 

Observation & Surprise: We were surprised that while we included Google/Facebook login for "convenience," P1 showed immediate resistance upon seeing these icons. 

Direct Quote: P1 said, "If this is a private XJTLU community, why is there a Facebook login? It makes me worry my student data will be sold." 

Insight: The demand for a "Verified Student Network" is much stricter regarding "purity" than we initially anticipated . 

Finding 2: Cognitive Load During Post Creation 

Observation & Surprise: During Task 3, P2 tried to cram a 50-word review entirely into the "Title" field because the "Content" box looked "too much like an optional notes section" without guidance. 

Direct Quote: P2 complained, "I don't know what to put where; the boundary between title and description is too blurry." 

• Insight: The lack of placeholder text led to significant data formatting errors . 

Finding 3: The "Visual Dead-End" 

Observation & Surprise: P5 attempted to "pinch-to-zoom" on a food photo three times to see menu details. When it failed to enlarge, they immediately closed the tab. 

Direct Quote: P5 said, "In 2026, not being able to click and expand a photo is unbelievable. I can't see the actual dish clearly." 

Finding 4: Navigation Disparity Across Devices 

Observation & Surprise: P3 (Mobile) struggled to find the "AI Smart Assistant" because it was buried in a bottom menu, whereas P6 (Web) found it instantly in the left sidebar . 

• Direct Quote: P3 noted, "The shifting icon locations make it feel like I'm using two different apps."

# Most Significant Change — Explained

Restructuring the Contribution Guidance: We initially thought "giving students total freedom" was best. Testing proved that freedom without guidance leads to chaos. By introducing Placeholder Text and Simplified Password Rules (6-digit alphanumeric) , we successfully reduced the average time to post from 120 seconds to 40 seconds, directly meeting the efficiency goals in our PRD. 

# What you chose not to change — and why

1. Map-Based Navigation: P4 and P6 suggested integrating a real-time GPS map. However, we chose not to implement this for the MVP. Reasoning: Our primary goal is to solve the 

"Information Vacuum" regarding food quality, not navigation. Since Taicang is a relatively selfcontained campus, students prioritize what to eat over how to get there. Adding a map would increase technical complexity without significantly improving the core 45-second discovery goal. 

2. Anonymous Posting: P1 asked if they could post reviews without logging in. We decided not to change our mandatory XJTLU login . Reasoning: The core value proposition of Taicang Bites is a "Verified Student Network". Allowing anonymous posts would risk the re-entry of "commercial bias" or "fake praise," which the project explicitly aims to eliminate. 

# Section 4 — Limitations

Sample Skew: While we tested 6 people, 5 were senior students. We lack sufficient data for "Freshmen" who are in their first week and totally unfamiliar with Taicang. 

Environment Bias: Sessions were held in quiet areas with strong Wi-Fi. We haven't tested "Real-world" scenarios (e.g., outdoor use with screen glare or weak 5G signals during lunch rushes). 

Long-term Retention: We validated "Single-task Success" but have yet to prove if the "Incentivized Contribution System" will actually drive users to post consistently over months.
